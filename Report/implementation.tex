\chapter{Implementation}
In this chapter, we will discuss our own implementation of a toy example of a gradient coding scheme. We will implement a simple gradient coding scheme based on the idea of polynomial interpolation as described in the reference paper. We will use Python for our implementation.
\section{Setup}
We have made an assumption in our implementation.
\begin{equation*}
    \text{n (Number of workers)} = \text{k (Number of datasets)}
\end{equation*}
This is consistent with the reference paper, which also makes a similar assumption for simplicity. 

We test the correctness of our implementation by trying out multiple test cases mentioned below:
\begin{table}[!h]
    \centering
\begin{tabular}{c|c|c|c|c|c}
\hline
\textbf{Test Case} & \textbf{n = k} & \textbf{d} & \textbf{s} & \textbf{m} & \textbf{l} \\ \hline
1 & 5 & 3 & 1 & 2 & 4 \\
2 & 6 & 3 & 1 & 2 & 6 \\
3 & 8 & 4 & 2 & 2 & 8 \\
4 & 10 & 5 & 3 & 2 & 20 \\
5 & 10 & 4 & 2 & 2 & 20 \\
6 & 10 & 3 & 1 & 2 & 20 \\
7 & 12 & 6 & 3 & 3 & 12 \\
8 & 15 & 5 & 2 & 3 & 15 \\ 
\hline
\end{tabular}
\caption{Test cases for our implementation}
\end{table}

n = number of workers, k = number of datasets, d = number of datasets assigned to each worker, s = number of stragglers tolerated, m = communication reduction factor, l = dimension of the gradient vector.

Once these test cases are passed, we can be confident that our implementation is correct and can be used for further experiments and analysis. After this we try to model the computation and communication time as shown in section 5 of the reference paper. We plot the modeled expected running times for our code and compare it with naive distributed schemes. The errors in the gradient recovery are also plotted for different values of n, s and m. 
% We also plot the condition number of the Vandermonde matrix used in our scheme to show how it affects the numerical stability of our scheme.

Values used for various plots:
\begin{itemize}
    \item n = k = 8 for runtime heatmap
    \item For expected runtime plot, we use d = 1, m = 1 for naive scheme, m = 1 and best d for m = 1 based on heatmap for Tandon scheme and $m \geq 2$ and best d, m pair from heatmap for the proposed scheme. Also $\lambda_1 = 0.8, \lambda_2 = 0.1$ and $t_1 = 1.6, t_2 = 6$ for the expected runtime.
    \item For communication cost vs straggler tradeoff plot, we use n = 12 and vary d and s values.
    \item For gradient error vs s plot, we use n = 10, l = 20 and vary s and m values, d = s + m in all cases.
\end{itemize}
